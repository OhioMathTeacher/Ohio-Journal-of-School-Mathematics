# Affordable Citation-Hallucination Detection: A Deterministic-First Approach

**Todd Edwards**
Ohio Journal of School Mathematics

---

## Why This Work Exists

On April 2, 2026, Nature published a feature article reporting that
2–6% of papers accepted at major computer science conferences in 2025
contained hallucinated citations — references that look plausible but
point to papers that do not exist.  An exclusive Nature analysis, in
collaboration with the commercial firm Grounded AI, estimated that more
than 110,000 of the 7 million scholarly publications from 2025 probably
contain invalid references generated by AI.

The article was thorough, well-sourced, and alarming.  It was also,
in several respects, ironic.

**Irony 1: The problem is reproducibility, but the analysis isn't
reproducible.**  Nature partnered with Grounded AI to analyze 4,000+
publications using a proprietary tool called Veracity.  Neither the
tool, the dataset, the risk-scoring model, nor the 20,000 synthetic
papers used to calibrate it were made publicly available.  The
methodology is described at a high level — enough to understand the
approach, not enough to reproduce it.  For an article about threats to
scientific integrity, the absence of reproducible methods is striking.

**Irony 2: The proposed solutions are gated behind the same barriers
the problem exploits.**  The article describes several detection tools:
Grounded AI's Veracity (commercial), GPTZero's screening tool
(commercial partnership with ICLR), Frontiers' in-house AI
(institutional), and CNRS's bibCheck (available only to CNRS
scientists).  The only open-source option mentioned — CheckIfExist — is
described in a single sentence.  The implicit message is: detection
requires either money or institutional affiliation.  Yet the researchers
most vulnerable to hallucinated citations — editors of small journals,
independent reviewers, scholars at under-resourced institutions — are
precisely the ones who lack both.

**Irony 3: The infrastructure to solve this already exists, for free.**
CrossRef indexes over 150 million DOIs.  OpenAlex covers 250 million
scholarly works.  Semantic Scholar, arXiv, and DataCite are all free
and public.  The Nature article itself relies on checking references
against these same databases.  The expensive part was never the data —
it was the assumption that you need AI to interpret it.

This project started the same week that article was published.  I read
it as the editor of a small mathematics education journal — exactly the
kind of journal with no budget for Grounded AI, no institutional
screening tools, and no defense against the problem Nature described.
The question was immediate and practical: **what can I build, right
now, with what's freely available?**

## What We Built

The Citation Validator is an open-source tool that checks academic
references against public databases using deterministic logic.  No
machine learning, no API keys, no subscription fees.  A reviewer
pastes a BibTeX bibliography into a web interface (or runs a
command-line script), and within minutes receives a per-citation
verdict: *valid*, *warning*, *suspicious*, or *invalid*.

The validation pipeline works in three tiers:

1. **DOI resolution.** If a citation includes a DOI, the tool resolves
   it through CrossRef (with DataCite and DOI.org fallbacks) and
   cross-checks the returned metadata — title, author, year — against
   the BibTeX entry.  A DOI that resolves to a different paper, or
   doesn't resolve at all, is strong evidence of fabrication.

2. **Database search.** Citations without DOIs are searched in OpenAlex
   (structured title filter) and Semantic Scholar (title search with
   author/year cross-validation).  If no matching record is found, the
   citation is marked *warning* (unverifiable) — not *suspicious*.
   This distinction is critical: many legitimate citations (arXiv
   preprints, non-English journals, older publications) are absent from
   these databases.  Absence of evidence is not evidence of fabrication.

3. **AI analysis (optional).** For users who want a second opinion, the
   tool can route citations through Gemini, GPT-4o, Claude, or Groq
   for AI-assisted judgment.  Gemini's free tier provides 1 million
   tokens per day — enough to analyze approximately 1,500 citations at
   zero cost.

The entire codebase, all test data, and all experimental results are
publicly available on GitHub — because a project about scientific
integrity cannot be taken seriously if it isn't itself transparent and
reproducible.

## What We Found

### The Tool Needed Auditing Too

**Irony 4: The detector itself needed detecting.**  A thorough code
review (by Claude Opus 4.6, an AI — more on that irony shortly) of
the ~6,000-line codebase revealed six issues, three of them critical:

- The **confusion matrix was inverted**: true positives and true
  negatives were swapped, meaning all accuracy metrics used
  non-standard definitions.  Every published number was wrong.
- Over **1,500 lines of dead code** (an abandoned browser-side
  validation pipeline) remained in production, including a
  contradictory AI prompt that instructed the model to "assume
  citations are REAL" — the opposite of the active prompt.
- The **escalation logic** treated "not found in any database" as
  evidence of fabrication, producing 100% false-positive rates on
  legitimate citations.  The validator was committing the same
  epistemological error it was designed to catch: treating the absence
  of a record as proof of something.

After fixes, the codebase shrank by 1,604 lines while gaining
correctness, a 19-test suite, and a proper evaluation framework.

### False-Positive Baseline

We tested the deterministic pipeline (no AI) against 391 real citations
from three sources:

| Dataset | Citations | Source | FPR |
|---------|-----------|--------|-----|
| arXiv CS preprints (2024) | 285 | Extracted from 2 published papers | 0.0% |
| CrossRef random sample | 96 | CrossRef random-works API | 0.0% |
| Nature article bibliography | 10 | Manual entry | 0.0% |
| **Total** | **391** | | **0.0%** |

Zero real citations were incorrectly flagged as suspicious or invalid.
The Wilson 95% confidence interval is [0.0%, 0.96%] — we can claim less
than 1% false-positive rate with 95% confidence, but not exactly zero.

These results should be interpreted with appropriate caution.  The test
set is small (391 citations), heavily weighted toward computer science
preprints, and does not yet include books, dissertations, non-English
publications, or very old works.  The planned 10,000-citation validation
study will address these gaps.  We are deliberately cautious here
because we are aware of the risk of overfitting to our test data — all
code changes addressed general design flaws, not specific test cases,
but the same team wrote both the code and the tests.

### What Remains

- **Fake-citation recall:** Re-run the 100-fake-citation dataset to
  confirm the escalation fix did not weaken detection of actual fakes.

- **AI comparison:** Run the same datasets with and without AI to
  quantify whether AI improves precision or whether deterministic
  checks are sufficient.

- **Scale validation:** A 10,000-citation study with diverse,
  independently-sourced datasets to confirm generalizability.

## The Ironies

This project is layered with contradictions worth naming explicitly.

**AI as disease and cure.**  The hallucinated-citation problem is caused
by large language models.  The code audit that found three critical bugs
in this project's detector was performed by a large language model
(Claude Opus 4.6).  The same class of technology that poisons citation
networks helped make this tool correct.  We do not pretend this tension
is resolved — but we note that refusing to use AI to fight AI-generated
problems would be like refusing to use a calculator to check arithmetic.
The question is not whether AI is involved, but whether its involvement
is transparent, auditable, and reproducible.  Ours is.

**The detector's own epistemological error.**  The original escalation
logic treated "not found in databases" as evidence of fabrication.  This
is precisely the error that makes hallucinated citations dangerous in
the first place — conflating plausibility with truth, or in this case,
conflating absence with falsehood.  The fix was not technical but
philosophical: absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.
Unverifiable is not the same as fake.

**A human citation error in a project about citation errors.**  During
testing, we discovered that our own BibTeX entry for Resnik & Hosseini
(2026) had the wrong title — "Fabricated references in academic
publishing" instead of the actual title, "Hallucinated citations
produced by generative artificial intelligence may constitute research
misconduct when citations function as data in scholarly papers."  The
validator correctly flagged it.  We were, briefly, our own false
positive.

**Open-source vs. proprietary, on a question of public trust.**  The
Nature article describes a threat to the shared infrastructure of
science — and then points toward proprietary, paywalled solutions.  If
hallucinated citations are a public problem, the detection tools should
be public goods.  Every dataset, every line of code, and every test
result in this project is freely available.  We believe that is not
incidental to the research — it is the research.

## Why It Matters

Citation hallucination is a solvable problem, and solving it should not
require an institutional budget.  The databases that index legitimate
scholarship — CrossRef, OpenAlex, Semantic Scholar — are free and
public.  The logic to query them is straightforward.  The hardest part
turned out to be getting the epistemology right: understanding that a
citation absent from databases is *unverifiable*, not *fake*.

If these preliminary results hold at scale, they suggest that the
academic community already has the infrastructure to detect most
hallucinated citations — we just need to use it.  An open-source tool
running on free APIs, requiring no AI and no budget, can be deployed by
any journal, any reviewer, any researcher, anywhere.

This is not just a tool.  It is a demonstration that generative AI can
be part of the solution — not only the problem.  And it is an
invitation: take the code, make it better, be a part of the solution.
