## Published Articles - Spring 2025 (Volume 99)

### 1. Using Generative AI to Reframe Mathematical Tasks for Personalized Learning
**Authors:** Theodora Beauchamp, Candace Walkington, Katie Bainbridge  
**Affiliations:** Southern Methodist University (Beauchamp, Walkington); OpenStax, Rice University (Bainbridge)  
**Citation:** Ohio Journal of School Mathematics, Issue 99, Spring 2025, pp. 6-18  
**Contact:** [To be added]

**PK-12 Focus:** ✓ **K-9 students** (elementary and middle school)

**Annotation:**  
This study examines how mathematics teachers use the MagicSchool AI platform to personalize word problems for K-9 students by incorporating student interests (sports, video games, popular culture). The research involves 12 teachers (3 preservice, 9 in-service) working with students from Kindergarten through Algebra 1. Teachers used three MagicSchool tools: Math Word Problem Maker, Make it Relevant, and Rewrite It to adapt standardized test problems and curriculum materials. The study addresses two research questions: (1) What themes arise as teachers explore benefits and drawbacks of generative AI for personalizing mathematics tasks? (2) How does teacher personalization through AI change the readability of mathematics tasks? Findings show that interest-based personalization through AI can boost student engagement and reduce math anxiety, though the study also identifies challenges and limitations of AI-driven personalization. This is highly relevant for PK-12 educators interested in practical applications of genAI for differentiation and student engagement.

### 2. High School Students View Snapchat AI to Be Like Google: Teacher Candidates Viewing Generative Artificial Intelligence in the Classroom
**Authors:** Haley M. Smiley, Amanda Gantt Sawyer, Juliana E. Galano  
**Affiliation:** James Madison University  
**Citation:** Ohio Journal of School Mathematics, Issue 99, Spring 2025, pp. 30-37  
**Contact:** [To be added]

**PK-12 Focus:** ✓ **High school students**

**Annotation:**  
Written from the perspective of teacher candidates observing in public schools, this paper documents how high school students actually use genAI tools in classrooms, particularly Snapchat's My AI (powered by ChatGPT). While many genAI platforms state their resources are not intended for users under 18, there are no formal restrictions preventing access. The paper presents observational case studies showing students bypassing school WiFi blocks by using cellular data to access social media platforms with embedded AI. The study highlights critical issues: genAI tools have difficulty with correct mathematical answers, produce biased responses, and create inappropriate content for younger audiences. The authors question whether students understand these limitations and are making informed discernments when using these tools. This is particularly valuable for PK-12 educators and administrators developing policies around student genAI use and for understanding the gap between how teachers intend technology to be used versus how students actually use it. The practitioner perspective makes this especially relatable for classroom teachers.

## Articles in Progress - Fall 2025 (Volume 101)

### 3. Exploring Potential Uses and Concerns of GenAI Use in Problem Solving in the Mathematics Classroom
**Authors:** Iris DeLoach Johnson, Zuhal Yilmaz, Nirmala Naresh, Deborah Cockerham  
**Status:** In Review  
**Citation:** Ohio Journal of School Mathematics, Issue 101, Fall 2025 (forthcoming)  
**Contact:** [To be added]

**PK-12 Focus:** ✓ **Secondary mathematics teachers** (in-service and preservice)

**Annotation:**  
This two-part longitudinal study explores the integration of generative artificial intelligence in mathematics education by examining the perspectives, knowledge, and experiences of in-service and preservice secondary mathematics teachers. The study began with a 7-item questionnaire completed by in-service secondary mathematics teachers in the West South-Central region of the United States to identify their initial views and concerns regarding GenAI. A volunteer subgroup participated in an exploratory task-based activity, completing one of eight structured mathematical problems using a GenAI tool. Insights from this work informed the second part: a case study involving preservice teachers who engaged in open-ended mathematical modeling tasks with GenAI. Analysis focused on teacher-AI interactions, including prompt types (Ask, Affirm/Seek Validation, Guide) and how interactions addressed concerns from previous research. The study examines teachers' reactions and beliefs regarding conceptual understanding supported during GenAI problem-solving experiences. Concludes with pedagogical reflections on challenges, limitations, and plans for future AI tool use. This research is valuable for understanding how mathematics teachers at the secondary level are adapting to and thinking critically about AI integration.

### 4. Utilizing AI to Create Mathematics-Themed Children's Stories
**Authors:** Ann Wheeler, Tiffany Sears Leach, Sarah Cooley  
**Status:** Copyediting  
**Citation:** Ohio Journal of School Mathematics, Issue 101, Fall 2025 (forthcoming)  
**Contact:** [To be added]

**PK-12 Focus:** ✓ **Elementary and middle school** (future teachers)

**Annotation:**  
This practitioner-based article describes an elementary classroom lesson utilizing two artificial intelligence resources, MagicSchool AI and ChatGPT, to help future elementary and middle school teachers write mathematics-themed children's stories. The authors provide lesson background information, student prompts, and sample children's stories demonstrating how AI can support creative integration of mathematical concepts into narrative form. The article discusses modifications for future classroom use and details teacher implications for implementing this approach. By focusing on children's literature as a vehicle for mathematics learning, this work bridges literacy and numeracy instruction while showcasing AI's potential role in supporting interdisciplinary teaching. Particularly useful for elementary educators and teacher preparation programs interested in innovative, engaging ways to present mathematical concepts through storytelling and for those exploring geometry concepts through creative writing.

### 5. Supporting Responsive Mathematics Teaching in Elementary Classrooms Using ChatGPT
**Authors:** [Selmer - first name to be confirmed]  
**Status:** In Review  
**Citation:** Ohio Journal of School Mathematics, Issue 101, Fall 2025 (forthcoming)  
**Contact:** [To be added]

**PK-12 Focus:** ✓ **Elementary classrooms** (prospective teachers)

**Annotation:**  
This study examines 28 prospective teachers' reported experiences and perceptions as they used a standardized prompt with ChatGPT-3.5 to analyze and respond to 56 elementary students' work on high cognitive demand mathematics tasks. Responsive mathematics teaching prioritizes understanding student thinking over scripted instruction, and teachers develop this skill through professional noticing of children's mathematical thinking—attending to, interpreting, and deciding how to respond to students' reasoning. The research addresses three questions: (1) How do prospective teachers experience ChatGPT's effectiveness in supporting the "attending to" and "interpreting" components of professional noticing? (2) What patterns emerge in the types of questions ChatGPT generates when responding to elementary students' mathematical work? (3) How do prospective teachers perceive ChatGPT's effectiveness in supporting the "deciding how to respond" component of professional noticing? This evidence-based study contributes to understanding how AI tools might act as pedagogical support to enhance prospective teachers' abilities to maintain cognitive demand and focus on student sense-making rather than simply correcting errors. Critical for teacher educators working with elementary mathematics methods students.

## University-Level Articles (Not PK-12 Focused)

### U1. Development and Application of a Chatbot for University-level Calculus Learning
**Authors:** [Park et al. - names from main.tex]  
**Status:** In Review  
**Citation:** Ohio Journal of School Mathematics, Issue 101, Fall 2025 (forthcoming)  
**Contact:** [To be added]

**Focus:** ✗ **University/College** (calculus students)

**Annotation:**  
This paper introduces an AI-powered calculus chatbot, "Solgit," designed to support college-level mathematics education through both in-class and self-directed learning. Using Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) technique, the chatbot provides accurate responses by linking subject-specific materials. The chatbot offers diagnostic tests, tailored precalculus reviews, and generates diverse calculus problems with solutions. It supports student-led learning by allowing instructors to assign weekly keywords for exploration. Students can study, organize concepts, and collaborate with classmates anytime. Practical examples demonstrate that this calculus chatbot enhances engagement and learning efficiency, showing the potential of subject-specific generative AI as an effective, affordable tool in higher education. The chatbot was developed first in Korean, but has potential to adapt to other languages. *While this article focuses on university-level mathematics, it may offer insights for advanced high school calculus courses or dual-enrollment programs.*

### U2. SMART Activities: Statistics Mnemonics Assembled with Reflection and Technology
**Authors:** Megan Mocko, [additional authors from main.tex]  
**Status:** Copyediting - Revision Round 2  
**Citation:** Ohio Journal of School Mathematics, Issue 101, Fall 2025 (forthcoming)  
**Contact:** [To be added]

**Focus:** ✗ **University/College** (business statistics students)

**Annotation:**  
This study presents results from using a three-part, multiday SMART (Statistics Mnemonics Assembled with Reflection and Technology) activity designed to help students in an introductory business statistics course explore mnemonic use with and without large language models (LLMs), to support memory retention and conceptual understanding. Preliminary findings from analysis of n=70 student reflections suggest that mnemonics created without the aid of LLMs felt more personal to students, while LLM-supported efforts helped students work more efficiently. Seventy-five percent of 108 statements about anxiety reported feeling a reduction in anxiety including having more confidence, feeling better prepared, and improved recall when the mnemonic exit ticket structure was used. The study examines how AI can support student learning while maintaining personal connection to content. *This university-focused research may have applications for AP Statistics courses or advanced high school statistics programs.*

### U3. Designing for Discernment: A Mixed-Methods Case Study of GenAI-Supported Critical Thinking
**Authors:** Yueyue Li, Jie Wu, Zheng Yang  
**Status:** In Review  
**Citation:** Ohio Journal of School Mathematics, Issue 101, Fall 2025 (forthcoming)  
**Contact:** [To be added]

**Focus:** ✗ **University/College** (undergraduate engineering students)

**Annotation:**  
Against the backdrop of rapid proliferation of generative AI tools bringing both opportunities and challenges to higher education, particularly in cultivating critical thinking within STEM fields, this paper investigates how integrating a GenAI tool into an undergraduate engineering course impacts students' critical thinking and teamwork, and examines strategies for instructors to facilitate ethical, effective use of AI in learning. In a one-semester case study involving 69 students, a novel "GenAI-CT" instructional model was deployed, integrating GenAI-driven activities with critical thinking exercises. Using a mixed-methods approach, quantitative tests gauged improvements in critical thinking skills, while qualitative analyses of student reflections and group interaction logs elucidated their experiences engaging with GenAI. The integration of GenAI was generally feasible and beneficial. Students showed improvement in certain critical thinking tasks (problem analysis and idea synthesis) and reported that GenAI support enriched group collaboration by offering timely feedback and diverse perspectives. However, significant challenges emerged—many students struggled with tasks requiring complex logical structuring even with AI assistance, and there was a tendency for some to over-rely on AI-generated answers without sufficient critical evaluation. The study introduces a GenAI-CT framework delineating ethical and pedagogical integration of AI tools into STEM education. *While focused on university engineering, the critical thinking framework and ethical considerations have relevance for advanced high school STEM programs.*

## Articles in Progress - Spring 2026 (Volume 102)

### 6. Practical Applications of AI in Teaching Mathematics for Elementary Teachers
**Authors:** Bridget Druken, Jason John McPheron  
**Status:** Pre-Publication  
**Citation:** Ohio Journal of School Mathematics, Issue 102, Spring 2026 (forthcoming)  
**Contact:** [To be added]

**PK-12 Focus:** ✓ **Preservice elementary teachers**

**Annotation:**  
This article examines how educators can use generative artificial intelligence in mathematics courses for preservice elementary teachers (PSTs). The authors provide strategies for AI integration in three areas: syllabus policy statements, planning support, and classroom activities. These strategies consider teaching innovation, practicality, and ethics. The article proposes four specific activities for AI-curious mathematics teacher educators: (1) drafting AI-focused syllabus policies, (2) generating example data for statistics and data science lessons, (3) summarizing PST discussions using ChatGPT, and (4) inviting PSTs to engage in teacher-student conversations about challenging and conceptually dense mathematical concepts. The authors discuss important limitations, including AI's ability to explain mathematical ideas conceptually, the limited use of visual outputs in text-based tools, and ethical considerations of using AI in teacher preparation. This practical resource is particularly valuable for mathematics teacher educators seeking concrete, implementable strategies for responsibly integrating AI into elementary methods courses while maintaining focus on conceptual understanding and pedagogical ethics.

### 7. From Prompt to Practice: Using Artificial Intelligence to Support Productive Struggle in Elementary Mathematics Methods Courses
**Authors:** Alesia Mickle Moldavan  
**Status:** Pre-Publication  
**Citation:** Ohio Journal of School Mathematics, Issue 102, Spring 2026 (forthcoming)  
**Contact:** [To be added]

**PK-12 Focus:** ✓ **Elementary teachers and K-12 classrooms**

**Annotation:**  
This study examines the use of an artificial intelligence tool (MagicSchool.ai) to support current and future elementary teachers in developing mathematical story problems, alongside the content knowledge and pedagogical strategies needed to facilitate problem-solving that encourages critical thinking, perseverance, and mathematical discussion. The research was conducted in elementary mathematics methods courses at a southeastern university: two asynchronous graduate sections for in-service teachers (n = 31) and two in-person undergraduate sections for preservice teachers (n = 46). Participants utilized AI tools to evaluate how their questioning techniques could enhance mathematical reasoning while maintaining cognitive demand and fostering productive struggle. Analysis of participants' course artifacts and reflections revealed four key ways AI tools strengthened instructional practice: (a) deepening understanding of mathematical processes and scaffolding, (b) improving question types and sequencing, (c) broadening exploration of diverse problem-solving approaches and opportunities for personalization, and (d) enhancing self-reflection in facilitating productive mathematical discussions. Findings highlight AI's potential as a prompt generator and question-scripting partner, with specific recommendations for integrating AI into teacher preparation and K-12 classrooms to promote reflective, effective mathematics instruction. Particularly valuable for understanding productive struggle as a pedagogical goal and how AI can support rather than undermine it.

### 8. AI as Collaborative Tool for Teachers for Effective Integration of Dynamic Geometry Tools
**Authors:** S. Asli Özgün-Koca, Michael Meagher, Todd Edwards  
**Status:** In Review  
**Citation:** Ohio Journal of School Mathematics, Issue 102, Spring 2026 (forthcoming)  
**Contact:** [To be added]

**PK-12 Focus:** ✓ **K-12 teachers** (fourth-grade example featured)

**Annotation:**  
This paper explores how AI can serve as a critical partner in developing technology-integrated materials for mathematics instruction, focusing on the iterative process of human-AI collaboration in creating GeoGebra applets. The authors present a case study beginning with a fourth-grade teacher scenario: students struggling with fraction concepts need interactive digital tools, but the teacher lacks GeoGebra expertise and time. Using the TPACK (Technological Pedagogical Content Knowledge) framework, the article examines how AI (specifically ChatGPT) can address barriers teachers face in creating dynamic geometry environments. The paper documents the collaborative development of two GeoGebra sketches—a fraction bar visualization and a base-5 multiplication model—detailing the iterative process of prompting AI, testing outputs, troubleshooting, and refining. The authors discuss AI's strengths in providing real-time feedback and technical guidance, as well as limitations requiring teacher expertise to evaluate and refine AI-generated code. Research shows that teachers often struggle with DGE technical proficiency, require collegial support, and face time constraints. AI offers potential as an on-demand collaborator for personalized assistance. This work is particularly valuable for K-12 mathematics teachers seeking to integrate dynamic geometry tools without extensive programming knowledge or access to tech-savvy colleagues, demonstrating practical strategies for leveraging AI as a professional development partner.
